Skills corner

Developing discernment

What makes an article credible? How can we assess its quality? Should we trust the findings?

  • Internal consistency - does the logic of the argument hold?

  • External consistency - are the arguments and data consistent with what is already known?

  • Craft - is the article nuanced and layered theoretically and empirically, paying attention to multiple factors?

  • Clarity - does the paper have a point of view and makes clear what it is adding to the conversation?

  • Fit - is there a reasonable connection between the argument raised and the methods used to test it?

Developing discernment

Potential correlates of quality

  • Journal of publication (?) - good articles may be too revolutionary; reviewers are an imperfect screen for errors

  • High citation counts (?) - citations for interest, rather than correctness

  • Predictive validity (?) - later work that replicates and extend findings; what about Newtonian mechanics?

  • Your judgment (?) - improves with experience; tempered by biases you may accumulate

Chamberlin (1965)

Key Points

  • While a particular theory focuses our attention when designing a study, we should not be blinded to alternative explanations

  • Alternative explanations should be “baked into” a study and given due consideration

  • Discriminating evidence is needed to distinguish between multiple plausible theories

Hypotheses: A method for accumulating knowledge

Knowledge accumulates through refining the theoretical statements at the top of the diagram, but it also accumulates as scholars cobble to- gether a better understanding of reality (Cart- wright, 1999) by improving their ability to de- velop and use representational models. Empirical support for hypotheses derived from a representational model demonstrates the use- fulness of that model in the proscribed context. (Harris, Johnson, and Souder 2013, 447)

Models: Styles of construction

  • Verbal theorizing: the standard method employed by most papers

  • Directed acyclic graphs (DAGs): becoming more common, even if the logic is operating in the background

  • Formal theory: use varies significantly by discipline

  • Subtle point: Each relationship in a model is technically a hypothesis; you elect to focus on certain relationships of interest (e.g., the novel ones)

Progressing your final project: Building a model

This week’s readings should help start launching you on a trajectory to convert your research question into a model with associated hypotheses

These hypotheses are often one of three flavors:

  1. Tests of relationships between constructs that you intend to perform (the standard empirical paper strategy)

  2. Relationships that are implied by a theoretical system that can be tested in the future (these are often called propositions)

  3. Assertions of the existance of a particular construct or mechanism (this is often the point of a qualitative piece)

Given the nature of this class, my preference is (1)
